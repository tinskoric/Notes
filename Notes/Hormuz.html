Everything is Cope

A Scattershot Summary, with References to Ukraine and Iran

Published

March 2026

1 No, new wars!

To close out the month of February, President Donald John Trump has, in his unyielding wisdom, blessed the world to a real treat. We are at war with Iran! Kind of. The United States, alongside its thoroughly loyal and perpetually steadfast ally, Israel, has successfully bombed a girls’ school. Other targets, of less pivotal strategic value, are soon to follow. This stroke of genius has been delivered by the “no new wars” administration (Greenhut 2026), because circumstances surely proved such a new war necessary. The President works in mysterious ways, and it is not our place to question the machinations of His genius. Nothing you say matters. Nothing that Trump said to the contrary before matters. All that matters is that we must be at war with Iran. The American military has wrought fire and epic fury upon the lands of the Iranian regime, and it is only a matter of time before American dominance is again cemented. Any day now.

This little text-scrawl largely focuses on the idea of “cope” in its various forms; with specific references to some American media, the Russo-Ukranian war, and the current American “war” with Iran. If you would rather skip right to the Iran part, go to Section 4. For a quick hint of what I think there: this is a stupid war that the United States will come out of having largely damaged or destroyed Iranian infrastructure and military equipment and basically nothing else. We will spend billions to blow up other peoples’ stuff because the State of Israel and the United States of America wanted to, and we will go home having further eroded our diplomatic standing. Trump will cite this destruction as victory with little input from the Iranians and enjoy his dollar-store triumph amid mass cope from his base and mass derision from everyone else. I do not think that this will go on for especially long (probably over by the end of March), and am certain that it will not turn into a major ground war because, despite all the bluster, even the Trump administration knows that that would be akin to suicide.

This is a massive waste of money (“FACT SHEET: How Much Is the War in Iran Costing Taxpayers?” 2026) in pursuit of… whatever this is in pursuit of.

2 A New Cope

Here is a fun exercise to know if a military operation is going well: count the number of times that the (stated) intended outcomes and investment have changed. At the onset of this conflict, the White House declared that it was seeking “unconditional surrender” from Tehran and expected the conflict to resolve quickly; within days. The various talking heads and lackeys of the MAGA1 movement, who for so long praised Trump’s strength as peace-maker, made sure to urgently parrot this more hawkish stance. Fast forward and the new line is that the President does not really need the Iranians to actually surrender, and can himself determine when the increasingly vague objectives of this operation have been met. In other words, Trump decides when Iran has “unconditionally surrendered.”

Trump alone is arbiter, wielding the power to determine when the war ends, no matter the agency of Iran. Nothing Iran says or does should matter to anyone, because, as we all know, Trump is in charge of everything. This is the kind of fantasy that one concocts when in denial. It is the exact kind of thing that would be expected of Trump in this exact circumstance, but that does not make it any less stupid. Imagine if you went under the knife for a knee replacement2, and after all the prior consultation and initial cost estimates, you woke up to find yourself with no new knee, a final cost that was quadruple what was promised, and a hospital administration that demanded you accept their narrative over any other; demanded you accept that your procedure never strictly promised a new knee. Better still, imagine that a sizable chunk of others who had undergone the same “procedure” did (ostensibly) accept that narrative, and did everything they could short of giving you a lobotomy such that you did too. This is what we call cope. This is the cope of a country that cannot reconcile the dissonance between its own reality and the true realities before it. The hospital is lying, the surgeon is an idiot too drugged to have operated on you, and the patients who still defend otherwise can be deemed only corrupt, drugged, or both.

2.1 The Base Fantasy

[…] every French citizen has to have forgotten the massacre of Saint Bartholomew.

— RENAN and GIGLIOLI (2018)

Cope, is a hell of a drug. In What is a Nation? Ernest Renan3 posits that something critical to national identity is “the act of forgetting” (RENAN and GIGLIOLI 2018). To be united as one, the many histories that divided the mass are forgotten; the past is forgotten, or reshaped to fit the present national identity. This demands a kind of cope from each participant. The victims of past violence are called to suppress their pains, and the perpetrators deny not only wrongdoing but ignore the event wholesale. The overall expectation is that every party understands that nothing worthwhile to their beings occurred, except that their “nation” victoriously endured.

Renan relies on this act of forgetting as a literal kind of thing. Of course, given that he knew of the massacre he cites, it is doubtful that Renan means to say that every French citizen has literally forgotten it. Rather, Renan means to say that, in the formation of the French identity, events like that are intentionally forgotten, and subsequently obscured, such that future generations do not dwell on them as sources of division. The act of forgetting to Renan is therefore a literal process of erasure, by which an illusion of a nation is constructed in the absence of knowledge. The modern nation is thus a fantasy4, buoyed by successive generations of cope. That such mental gymnastics are possible, and common, and integral to national development, should make certain to us that they are not limited to the concept of nationhood. Moreover, it is worth questioning how nations survive where knowledge is available.

In The Sublime Object of Ideology, Slavoj Žižek cites Marx’s notion of the illusion of “commodity fetishism”, the way that social relations among peoples appear as relations among things, and connects this to what he calls the ideological fantasy. Money is no more than a set of social relations, but “to the individuals themselves, this function of money—to be the embodiment of wealth—appears as an immediate natural property of a thing called ‘money’” (Zizek 1989). Money is a placeholder for value, but you hold it in your hands. Žižek writes that the classic notion would assume this perception a function of knowledge, and that the illusion resides in the space between individuals and that knowledge; meaning, one does and believes, because they do not know. Rather, Žižek asserts that the place of the illusion is not in knowledge, but in reality itself. It is not that people do not know, and thus do, but that they recognize the illusion and do anyways. This process creates dissonance between the reality we experience and the true kernel of reality that belies it. This kernel is the real, and it is what is truly experienced beneath all illusion. To maintain our reality thus requires not only the illusion, but also overlooking that it is an illusion. This is the ideological fantasy; the bridge between the real and our reality.

He knows very well that Roman law and German law are just two kinds of law, but in his practice, he acts as if the law itself, this abstract entity, realizes itself in Roman law and German law.

— Zizek (1989)

The ideological fantasy is not necessarily things as you believe they should be, but rather how you permit yourself to perceive them. You may well be an anarchist at heart, but you still recognize stupid laws and follow them because you “have to.” To go further, you do not even need to “like” your reality; it only needs to be coherent to you. The hidden discomforts of the truly real are blanketed by the relative comforts of ideological fantasy, a fantasy that gives us meaning and simplifies the gaps of the truly real. It is compartmentalization for a real that is even more complex than the reality we perceive. You know that there are no particularly special natural properties to money, but knowing that does not help you in your reality.

Thinking back to nationhood through this lens, what would learning of the massacre of Saint Bartholomew do to the typical French citizen? Would such revelatory knowledge call them to strip themselves of citizenship and renounce their French-ness? Unlikely. The act of forgetting to us is therefore not literal; you can know of these events, and learn of them, and still have “forgotten” them. Your participation is unconscious, and you are not shielded from it by your knowledge or cynicism5. Your willingness to take part is the fantasy. As Žižek notes, “[…] even if we do not take things seriously, even if we keep an ironical distance, we are still doing them” (Zizek 1989). Even your cynicism is cope; it is a comforting fantasy to think that your public cynicism means anything to your reality.

Ideological fantasy is cope; it is the base cope. To reject fantasy is to reject living in your reality. But sometimes, it gets a little too stupid.

2.2 Layered Cope

It is the awareness that we live in an insulated artificial universe which generates the notion that some ominous agent is threatening us all the time with total destruction.

— Žižek et al. (2009)

Part of the ideological fantasy being in reality, and not in the place of knowledge, is that we know about it. Most everyone understands that their world is “fake” on some scale, and that knowledge is, of course, deeply discomforting in itself. That is why we overlook it. But sometimes, it may become especially difficult to do so. In such cases, it is still easier to retreat to the worlds of other fantasies to “explain” the failures of your own, than to reject fantasy entirely. Once you can no longer suppress the discomforts of the real, it becomes easier to believe in a reality of conspiracy than to reject reality itself. This kind of thing drives people insane.

In Welcome to the Desert of the Real!, Slavoj Žižek, opens saying that the “ultimate American paranoiac fantasy” is to be effectively Truman Burbank in The Truman Show (Žižek et al. 2009); slowly unraveling that the society around him is a farce. This particular story uniquely appeals to the American sentiment because our society is one that has thoroughly commodified itself, but its basic conspiratorial nature is one that appeals globally.

We know that we live in fantasy, and know that we overlook it to continue living. When we struggle with fantasy, it is easiest to fantasize about unraveling it to reveal another. The fantasy exists to simplify the real, and when it fails we look for something simpler. What simpler is there but the notion that one person or group is the source of our struggles? These paranoiac fantasies, and all such conspiratorial views, are cope. Cope on top of cope.

Vitally, whatever their appearance, these conspiratorial views do not exist in direct conflict to the base fantasy, but rather are layered atop it. You may believe that there is a shadowy Illuminati-like organization that controls all currencies, but you still need to spend money to live and recognize its “natural” value in your reality. This line of thought serves the same function as our cynicism, or any other such cope. It exists to let you push away thoughts of the real, plugging gaps that emerge in it or in other layers of fantasy.

No matter the expression, we invent new ways to cope with the real. Either way, we do not leave. It is all cope, and all copes can be happening at the same time. That they all happen can also create its own dissonance; typically because new layers can become increasingly outlandish.

2.3 It’s All So Tiresome.

By this point, it is worth observing that this is all exhausting. Think of the exact arguments you have had with people who have deeply different realities from your own, admit to their fantasy, and then retreat to ironic nonsense or conspiracy or some other thing to weasel from deeper reflection. Now think of how you do the same thing; not because you want to be annoying or exhausting, but because it is so tiresome already to live in your reality without confronting the real. That exhaustion you feel is the strain of various layers of cope, all in tension to maintain your perception. Even the expression of burn-out is arguably a new aspect of cope. Everyone is burned-out, and everyone is saying that. Bosses will recognize your burn-out, and offer sympathy if they are so kind, but you will still be working6.

Part of this exhaustion is that these layers are being added with increasing frequency. States and societies have reached levels of connection and abstraction that were hardly imaginable in the pre-modern world, and that development forces us to constantly approach the illusion in ways that we do not want to. The less that you can feel the human on the other side, the more deeply fake everything feels. As outlined earlier, the illusion is maintained by our willingness to overlook it, but with so much knowledge on the apparent “laws” of our fantasies, and the overt recognition of just how little they are followed, we must construct more and more layers to maintain coherence. It is exhausting to be constantly reminded that nothing means anything. In the above clip7, a viewer is asking about Trump being the “Messiah ben Joseph,” and Fuentes is, in a rare show of reason, exhausted by such a question. Who the hell would seriously believe that Trump is the Jewish messiah, descendant of Joseph?8 To say, “it wasn’t like this before,” as Fuentes does, is of course a fantasy in itself; imagining that things used to work in ways that they do not now. But it is arguably true that the illusion was easier to overlook in the days before “everything’s a conspiracy.” The “5D chess” line references another cope, which is that political figures (let us say, Trump), who behave in ways contrary to their rhetoric, are merely making complex political moves, and not betraying their base. Of course they are betraying their base! But when you are of that base you would rather believe otherwise. At the same time, if someone comes to you, and puts on this mask talking about “oh it’s 5D chess man, you just don’t get it!” you would be forgiven for finding them exhausting.

Fuentes saying that he has to “become liberal” hearkens to the idea that perhaps the grass is greener in the worlds of other realities; that the fantasy is easier to overlook, in corners with seemingly fewer layers of cope. It is not, and this is a fantasy too. Importantly, while we are acutely exhausted today, this exhaustion is not especially new.

2.4 Disillusion as Cope

Sergeant Prendergast: Now let’s go meet some nice policemen. They’re good guys. Come on, let’s go.

Bill Foster: I’m the bad guy?

Prendergast: Yeah.

Bill Foster: How did that happen? I did everything they told me to.

— Falling Down (Wikiquote)

The success of the United States in the Cold War, and its place (briefly) as the sole “hyperpower” of the globe, enabled Americans to build a world of total illusory comfort. If there is a place in the world where ideological fantasy is strongest, it is here; the heart of the new global order. In fact, the real has become so abstracted by these comforts that its kernel is rather the uncomfortable knowledge of the illusion more than anything else. This is not because the United States has successfully mitigated the ails of the real, but rather that its people have become so drunken to the cope. Everyone has this knowledge, but the American is uniquely discomforted by it because their illusion is so strong; akin to the paranoiac fantasy in Žižek et al. (2009). That exact and unique discomfort, in the immediate post-Soviet world is what gives rise to stories like The Truman Show, but also Office Space and Falling Down. Where the former reflects this paranoaic fantasy of unraveling reality, the latter two present a fantasy where its characters have already become disillusioned with their realities, and are exacting their revenge. That in each case they find “success” in their efforts speaks to a larger power fantasy for Americans without meaning.

Where victory had granted its staunchest believers no more secure a reality, and the social gaps once excused by fear of Soviet aggression were on full display, a cope for such disillusion was a fantasy of rebellion against an indifferent reality. It is at this same time that we see the modern world and its enormity come into focus; where each nation has developed so much bureaucracy that it becomes nigh-impossible to recognize the person on the other side. There is something that became less personal.

William Foster in Falling Down is a man at the end of his rope. He represents a stereotypical kind of white-collar working man, now unemployed and devoid of meaning. After brushing against increasing exhaustion, he breaks and goes on a rampage. In his exploits, he is neither particularly cruel nor especially righteous, but can be best described as someone who wants to feel treated with respect and given purpose; someone who wants to be seen and observed for all his presumed toils in life. Foster just wants things to “work” as he imagines they should. He obeys certain norms but rages over the various small nuisances of his world. Overall, Foster is a man who has become thoroughly disillusioned in his fantasy and is enraged by its incongruities. It is no wonder that such a story of lost purpose would so soon follow close of the Cold War.

Foster has no victory in life. He embodies the anger of a person that has observed continuous personal loss despite the reported success of his nation9. He has observed all the norms of his reality, yet he is hardly rewarded for his efforts. He is unemployed, struggling, and treated with suspicion when requesting seeking basic courtesy. In his break, Foster responds by being sufficiently violent to make the reality around him operate as it should in his. That this response largely works (until his demise), fulfills both a power and revenge fantasy for the viewer; but it remains a fantasy.

To imagine yourself as a Foster-like character, who could go on such a rampage and believes that doing so would imbue them with some kind of recognition, is a cope. You are not going to be Foster. You are going to continue operating in the illusion, and when you close your eyes and imagine yourself as Foster (or a character smashing a printer in Office Space), it serves only to help continue distracting you from the illusion. The violence of these fantasies should not be overlooked.

Americans coming out of the Cold War are thus best understood as almost-aimless sore winners, left to confront their own societies and being deeply unhappy with them. In time, as American foreign policy was left free without a Soviet check, it would be that we “became” the world’s policeman; our various troubles on the homefront being relegated again to the righteous cost paid to our effort. In this role, the American found meaning again. While the United States would of course aim to expand its new de facto monopoly on international law10, becoming the powerful and nosy state we see now, its justifications gave the American psyche purpose.

3 Russia’s Cope-doggle

The Russians are not so fortunate as the Americans; not so fortunate to have been raised as victors of the 20th-century. Russians instead had the unique honor of watching their state be reduced to its smallest size in centuries11. The years of Yeltsin’s “shock therapy” resulted in former Soviet bureaucrats becoming wealthy oligarchs, mafia organizations assuming immense local power, and an overall prestige hit worthy of a mighty state thrown to the gutter. This experience was understandably deeply traumatic to the Russian psyche, and has thus resulted in a highly revanchist culture that, one way or another, wants to be of first-rate importance again.

3.1 Perverse Underdogs

Just as the United States, the nature of the Russian state is to seek power. The cope of the Russian people, is useful to that end. This cope is best-surmised as Soviet and imperial aesthetics without Soviet and imperial policy. The truth of the Russian state is that it is an oligarchic republic with its respective interests balanced carefully by a corrupt but firm federal administration. The illusion of Russia, is that President Vladimir Putin is a spy-genius-turned-dictator, feared by the “West,” who will return to her her lost prestige. Further, that Russia is in her present position not due to failures of the past, but because she has been intentionally hobbled by the West, and stripped of her rightful historic lands. It is not that the Soviet system became increasingly fragile and collapsed under tensions that pained the United States too, because that is less comforting than the idea that a “group” is to blame. This is obvious cope. To know that you lost the Cold War without it ever going hot and continue asserting your “claims” to lands that do not want you is cope. Moreover, to think that your military (“the Russian bear”) is particularly deserving of fear or respect today only appeals to the minds of people who have looked away from its repeated failures. The First Chechen War should have been a definitive rebuke of any dream of Russian military dominance.

When the Russian Federation escalated its own military enterprise in Ukraine, state media reported that the war would be quick and decisive. Somehow this is still an underdog narrative for Russia, despite Ukraine being one-quarter the population. Although it was not Putin himself, but rather his Belorussian counterpart Lukashenko who said it (2022), Russian talking heads parroted the notion of the war being over in as little as 3 days.

3.2 Someone Eventually Won the Great War

The war in Ukraine has now gone on for longer than the siege of Leningrad, and the frontline has been roughly as static throughout. The number in dead and wounded and the investment otherwise has far exceeded the vague promises of a “quick war.” The truth is that Russia is “winning,” if only in the sense that Ukraine is surely losing, but that this victory is exceptionally slow, bloody, and demonstrably contingent on the decline of American and European support (of Ukraine). However you will find little mention of this mounting struggle from much of the Russian citizenry.

That their military is not as potent as they believe does not mean that Russia is weak. One unique aspect of the Russian cope, is that it is born from failure rather than victory. This distinction from American cope is valuable, as an “underdog” story gives more room to tolerate losses incurred later. America is not only the victor of the 20th-century, but is also largely safe from most threats to it. Russia, by contrast, is not so lucky. Thus, akin to the Soviet mentality of the [Great Patriotic War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Patriotic_War_(term)), no cost is too high. As such, against the wishes of her neighbors and former peers, the Russian people have, in their cope, endeavored to reclaim their lost prestige, and are willing to do so at great cost. In Ukraine, Russia has made notable, if small, gains in the past year (2024 to 2025) compared to other years of the conflict, and that is largely explained by their resolve to continue.

That Russia initiated this war was stupid, and the result of cope. To think that the Ukrainian state would not raise effective resistance was stupid. To think that the Russian armed forces were of the class that could pull off moves like paradrops at Hostomel Airport was stupid. To think that the Ukrainians, who have spent the better part of a decade improving their forces in response to earlier Russian meddling, could not respond to Russian air power or strikes, was stupid. In essence, to believe that this would turn into anything but a difficult slog was stupid. But, in the end it is likely that Russia will still come away with at least the lands it “annexed” already, because the Russians will simply not leave otherwise. This war is a boondoggle, but Russia will come out of it with something tangible. The same cannot be said for the Americans!

4 A War with Iran is Really Stupid (That’s why it’s not happening)

The United States of America cannot win this war with the Islamic Republic of Iran. This does not mean that it is impossible to concoct a scenario where the United States would win12, but rather that there is no way that America would even fight the kind of war where it would win. Iran is not a small country, nor is it a weak one that could be treated as some sideshow in a real ground war. Iran has a larger population than Nazi Germany, more modern production methods, and extremely favorable terrain. Do you think that the United States is prepared to augment its economy, even a little, as it did in the most deadly conflict in human history?

Knowing that such a conflict is effectively unwinnable, the question of why the United States and Israel would initiate it, and what the likely outcomes will be, are pretty open. To both of these questions, we can simply respond with “cope.”

4.1 Post-9/11

Continuing from our place in Section 2.4, the United States spent the 1990s onward engaged as a “policeman” in various theaters across the globe. Such behavior (including similar endeavors before that decade), understandably, deeply upset certain peoples in those theaters, culminating in the September 11th terror attacks. I would hesitate to be so crass, but Žižek et al. (2009) describes this as a kind of wish-fulfillment on the part of Americans; the police getting their crime. The cruelty unleashed in the aftermath of those attacks is reflective of a deeply violent society that had found its excuse. In addition to invading the Islamic Emirate of Afghanistan in search of the perpetrators of 9/11, the United States also invaded Iraq; Iraq had nothing to do with the attack, and one million Iraqi civilians died as a result of American intervention. The United States justified this by grouping several nations of varying inter-state enmity together under the “Axis of Evil,” asserting that they together sponsored global terror. Iraq and Iran were both the core members of this grouping, but the United States invaded only the former. In either case, the American public was poised to expect decisive victory where we did engage; we were, of course, the world police.

Iraq and Afghanistan became costly boondoggles and the United States eventually lost both; losing the latter after an especially prolonged stay. Both cases illustrated that our position as the force with all the shiny toys was fading (Arquilla 2010), and that the American resolve for prolonged conflicts was weak. These were indeed costly and long wars, but in truth the daily experience of most Americans was hardly affected by their continuation. Still, the American variety of cope did not have the stomach for such things. The immediate reckoning of this was that the administration following that of President George W. Bush Jr., that of Barack Obama, opted to scale back certain commitments and accept a slightly more limited role globally (Krepinevich 2009). All the while, Americans coped by focusing on the loss against insurgency, rather than the failure of invasion13; ignoring that the result is the same loss. These conflicts began what would become a trend for American “war” in the 21st-century: bomb people you do not like, maybe send some relatively small troop deployments, then go home to the safety of North America after having sustained minimal casualties and declare some kind of victory.

The last thing the United States wants is another outright loss to have to cope its way around; it is easy to call yourself the strongest when you pick fights with toddlers.

4.2 Planning

The United States knows, that a war with Iran is effectively unwinnable, particularly given the style of “war” that has been adopted (McCabe 2016; “"U.s. Joint Forces Command Millennium Challenge 2002: Experiment Report",” n.d.). That is precisely why, despite all our national rhetoric against the Iranians, we had not launched such a war. In 2002, the United States launched the Millennium Challenge 2002, a war game exercise against a totally unknown country in the Persian Gulf. In this exercise, the United States lost, until it rewrote the rules to win (“"U.s. Joint Forces Command Millennium Challenge 2002: Experiment Report",” n.d.). Despite this enormous waste of $250 million, it is clear that somewhere in the last few decades to now, the American defense apparatus understood that a war with Iran would be difficult, if not entirely unwinnable; and most assuredly advised each presidential administration of exactly that.

Iran has 90 million people, a sizable military, domestic manufacturing capacity, deeply favorable terrain, and a key bargaining chip in the Strait of Hormuz. It is a total waste of time to fight a real war there and everyone knows that. The cope of American pure might, and some perceived weakness of Iran, did not outweigh that reality until now. The United States simply is not as strong as we may perceive it; if it were, this war would have happened earlier and been won. This should not need much explanation.

4.3 Trump and This “War”

To understand is difficult; to act is easy.

— Sun Yat-sen

We have already described the American psyche in detail, and while it has been very cursory, we have explained why victory in a war with Iran would doubtful. This should tell us exactly what has happened and what will be the outcome. This is not a “war” any more than the (more brief) exchange of fire last summer was. There simply will not be Americans landing on the shores of Iran, because that is not practical. Rather, Trump intends to bomb Iran into doing his nebulous bidding, which will not happen. As this does not happen, Trump will eventually stop and declare victory anyways. I envision that backroom talks will boil down to bombing Iran until the strait is re-opened and declaring victory when that occurs. Given that the Iranian navy will be at least partly sunk while the United States retains its fleet, the cope-addled conclusion will thus be that the United States must have won. The White House has already begun preparing for this by releasing a “timeline” detailing their much more limited intentions, despite those obviously conflicting with Trump’s own statements these past weeks.

Let us start to close here. When you see Trump oscillating between asking for help from scorned allies and defiantly declaring that we need no help, and when you see his core sycophantry cheer for some imagined power, just remember that it is cope. Given its own vague nature as “the reality you perceive” there are many different ways to interpret the rhetoric leading up to this conflict, and doing so in proper depth is beyond my tolerance for scribbling. Broadly, this war reflects a violent nation that desperately wishes to believe itself a still uniquely powerful and feared one when instead it is increasingly derided for its poor behavior on the world stage. Of course, this nation is still unbelievably powerful otherwise, which is why it remains safe enough geopolitically to “get” to act so poorly. America holds both the capacity to do stupid things, and experiences a lack of consequence such that its only limit is what it thinks it could cope its way around.

Trump knows he needs help but would sooner die than admit it, and his base knows this is stupid, but wishes it was not. What is real is that this war further erodes our standing with our partners and only serves to make us despised. The American decline will continue until shareholder value improves14.

5 What More is There

There is nothing but cope in the world. You already know it, and you are already aware of it, just accept it. I give the war in Ukraine another couple months to a year before some kind of settlement; either “ending” it or just offering a robust ceasefire. I give the war with Iran until the end of March. Maybe that is just cope too.
